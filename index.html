Why Did Georgia and Armenia Experience the Greatest Impact from COVID-19?

It is evident that there is a weak correlation between the percentage of the population that died from COVID-19 and the percentage of the population that tested positive for the virus. By utilizing a bubble chart, we can identify the primary factors influencing the spread of COVID-19:

  1. The total share of fully vaccinated individuals.
  2. Gross domestic product (GDP) at purchasing power parity.
  3. Diabetes prevalence (percentage of the population aged 20 to 79) in 2017.
  4. The share of the population with basic handwashing facilities on premises.
  5. Hospital beds per 1,000 people, using the most recent available data since 2010.
  6. A composite index measuring the average achievement in three fundamental dimensions of human development: a long and healthy life, knowledge, and a decent standard of living.

The data spans three years: 2020, 2021, and 2022.

Useful insights indicate that Georgia experienced a fatality rate of 0.45% and a positive case rate of 48%, while Armenia had a fatality rate of 0.31% and a positive case rate of 35%, both of which are higher than in other countries. Most countries fall into the third quadrant of the chart. Notably, Georgia and Armenia exhibit lower rates of vaccination, GDP, and hospital beds per thousand people. Additionally, both countries are members of the Commonwealth of Independent States.

Is There a Correlation Between Age and COVID-19?

By examining a scatter plot, we can explore the relationship between the share of the population aged 65 and older (x-axis) and the share of the population with confirmed COVID-19 cases (y-axis).

The scatter plot indicates a positive correlation, suggesting that countries with a higher proportion of elderly individuals tend to have a bigger share of confirmed COVID-19 cases among their population. Conversely, countries with a larger percentage of COVID-19 cases relative to their population also tend to have a higher proportion of older individuals.

However, it's important to note that there are some outliers. For instance, countries like Brunei, with a relatively low percentage of the population aged 65 and above (4.59%), exhibit a high percentage of confirmed COVID-19 cases (59.42%). Similarly, Bahrain shows a significant number of cases but a relatively smaller elderly population.

Japan, on the other hand, stands out with a substantial proportion of older individuals, accounting for 27% of the population, and a significant share of COVID-19 cases (23%).

Overall, while there is a general trend indicating a correlation between age and COVID-19 cases, the presence of outliers and unique country-specific factors should be considered for a comprehensive analysis.

Controlling the Virus: Analyzing Government Lockdown Measures

In order to assess the impact of lockdown measures implemented by governments across various Asian countries, a Choropleth Map is utilized. This map provides a visual representation of how these measures have evolved over time.

Choropleth maps are effective tools for depicting the distribution and intensity of a specific attribute or frequency within a geographic area, using varying colors or shades.

To evaluate the strictness of the government response, a composite score known as the Stringency Index was assigned to each country by researchers at Oxford University. A higher score indicates a more rigorous response, with a maximum score of 100 representing the strictest measures. The Stringency Index is calculated based on nine key metrics:

  1. School closures
  2. Workplace closures
  3. Cancellation of public events
  4. Restrictions on public gatherings
  5. Closures of public transport
  6. Stay-at-home requirements
  7. Public information campaigns
  8. Restrictions on internal movements
  9. International travel controls

By analyzing the Choropleth Map and considering the Stringency Index, we can gain insights into the effectiveness and extent of lockdown measures implemented by governments to combat the spread of the virus in different Asian countries.
